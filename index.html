What is CMIP6?

The Coupled Model Intercomparison Project Phase 6 (CMIP6) is the world's most comprehensive collection of climate simulations, coordinated by over 40 institutions worldwide. These models project how our climate will evolve under different Shared Socioeconomic Pathways (SSPs) — scenarios that describe possible futures based on emissions, policy, and societal choices.

The Four Pathways

CMIP6 models climate under four Shared Socioeconomic Pathways (SSPs). Each represents a different future — driven by the choices we make today about emissions, energy, and policy.

🌱 SSP1-2.6 — "The Green Path"

What it means today: Rapid shift to renewable energy, global cooperation, net-zero by 2050. Aligned with the Paris Agreement.

By 2100: ~1.5–2°C warming. Manageable impacts, most ecosystems preserved.

📊 SSP2-4.5 — "Middle of the Road"

What it means today: Current policies continue, some mitigation efforts, uneven progress. Emissions peak around 2050.

By 2100: ~2.5–3°C warming. Significant impacts, but worst outcomes avoided.

⚠️ SSP3-7.0 — "Regional Rivalry"

What it means today: Resurgent nationalism, trade wars, countries prioritize domestic fossil fuels over global cooperation.

By 2100: ~3–4°C warming. Severe regional disparities, Arctic warms 4× faster.

🔥 SSP5-8.5 — "Business as Usual"

What it means today: Unchecked fossil fuel use, no meaningful climate policy. Rapid economic growth powered by coal, oil, and gas.

By 2100: ~4–5°C warming. Catastrophic impacts to ecosystems, agriculture, and coastal cities.

These aren't predictions — they're "what if" stories. The difference between the green and red paths is entirely about choices we make this decade.

Present-Day Warming

Warming is already here. Between 2015 and 2024, global average temperatures have risen ~0.3–0.9°C above the 1995–2014 baseline, with the Arctic warming several times faster. Precipitation patterns are also shifting — some regions are getting wetter, others drier.

Precipitation change under SSP5-8.5 by 2100

SSP5-8.5: Business as Usual

If emissions continue unabated (the "fossil-fueled development" path), global temperatures could rise 4–5°C by 2100. The Arctic would warm over 15°C. This scenario represents the upper bound of possible climate outcomes — and the stakes couldn't be higher.

Global temperature scenarios over time

SSP3-7.0: Regional Rivalry

In this high-emissions scenario, warming reaches ~3–4°C globally by 2100. But the impacts are far from uniform. The Arctic warms more than four times the global average, while tropical regions face combined heat and precipitation stress.

Projected 2100 warming by region

SSP2-4.5: Middle of the Road

Under this "middle path" scenario — where current policies continue but some mitigation is adopted — warming stabilizes around ~2.5–3°C. The world still warms significantly, but many of the worst outcomes are avoided.

SSP1-2.6: The Green Path

With aggressive emissions reductions and a shift toward sustainability, warming could be held to ~1.5–2°C. This is the pathway aligned with the Paris Agreement goals. It requires rapid decarbonization — but the data shows it's still within reach.

Initial Prototype Writeup

What we have built so far

Current progress

Team Vizionary has built the foundation for an explorable explanation of CMIP6 climate projections. So far, we have prepared a climate data pipeline covering near-surface air temperature and precipitation across four Shared Socioeconomic Pathways: SSP1-2.6, SSP2-4.5, SSP3-7.0, and SSP5-8.5. The processed data includes global and regional time series from 2015 to 2100, summary statistics for eight regions, a TopoJSON world basemap, and gridded JSON exports for late-century map views. We also created six static exploratory visualizations to frame the story, including global temperature and precipitation maps, scenario comparison lines, regional warming bars, a temperature-precipitation scatter plot, and a regional heatmap. On the website, we have implemented the main scrollytelling structure with a full-screen hero, a sticky visualization panel, scroll-triggered narrative steps, and a final takeaway section.

Design challenge ahead

The hardest part of the final project will be designing the interactive geographic view and connecting it to the rest of the story. Climate model output starts as high-dimensional gridded data, so we need to translate it into web-friendly formats that load quickly while still preserving meaningful regional patterns. The choropleth map must support scenario changes, temperature and precipitation toggles, readable color scales, hover tooltips, and click-based region selection without overwhelming the viewer. The linked-view interaction is especially challenging because a selected region should update the map, line chart, bar chart, and other supporting views consistently. We also need to balance scientific nuance with clarity, making sure users understand that the difference between low- and high-emissions pathways is not abstract: it changes how much warming regions experience and how precipitation patterns shift by 2100. Our final design goal is to make the data feel explorable while still guiding visitors toward the main takeaway that the future depends on choices made now.

The future isn't written yet.

Every fraction of a degree of warming prevented matters — especially for the Arctic, which warms four times faster than the global average, and for regions already on the edge of water security. The choice between SSP1-2.6 and SSP5-8.5 is the difference between a world we can adapt to and one we cannot.
